01. DEPARTURE

The Amputation

When I got my business class ticket I was over the moon; but when it was "The" day to leave my home, it slowly felt not a luxury; rather, an anesthesia. I sat in the lounge surrounded by polite silence, trying to numb the sensation of leaving. For someone leaving the Global South, travel isn't movement—it's surgery. Surgery of my character and what I am.

It all started when I saw the migrant workers of my country in Doha, but I didn't speak to them. I built a wall of class to separate us. I am not sure why I did that? Maybe I was embarassed myself or I didn't want them to feel embarassed?

02. ARRIVAL

The Violence of Silence

In Bath, the silence was deafening. Back home, noise is life. Here, the silence felt judgmental. It wasn't peaceful; it was terrifyingly orderly.

I found myself walking softer, attempting to shrink myself. The order of the First World is beautiful, but it is also a form of containment. I walk the same streets everyday, but I have not even smiled at anyone while walking down the streets. This is so different back home in Nepal, where everyone knew each-other and stopping to chat with someone was a norm. Here, people are busy with their own life and I am busy trying to make distinction on which country is better --Nepal or UK.

03. ECONOMICS

The £4 Sandwich

For a month, my mind was a currency converter. I bought a sandwich, and my brain screamed: "That is two days' wages back home." The food tasted like ash.

Slowly, however, the mental calculator quieted down. I began to understand that navigating this economic disparity was part of the education itself. I wasn't just learning engineering/arts/science; I was learning the visceral reality of global inequality. That bitter taste of the sandwich taught me more about the economy than a textbook could. It gave me a resolve that isn't just about passing exams, but about eventually bridging the gap between the economy I spend in and the one I call home.

04. INFRASTRUCTURE

The Pavement

I became obsessed with the floor. The stones in Bath were so flat. It made me furious. Why was walking without tripping a luxury back home in Nepal?

My anger wasn't about stones. It was about the injustice of "baseline." Here, safety is the baseline. Home, it is a radical demand. For a long time, this contrast made me bitter. But eventually, I found a way to let these two realities coexist. The smooth stones of Bath remind me of what is possible; the memory of the uneven ground reminds me of what is necessary. I learned to walk on the 'luxury' of this pavement without losing the grit I developed from tripping. I am no longer just angry at the difference; I am motivated by the possibility of one day erasing it.

05. CLIMATE

The Ceiling

In Nepal, the sky is vast and blue. In England, the sky is a low, grey ceiling pressing on your shoulders. Initially my optimism was solar-powered. Without the sun, I had to manufacture hope artificially. I missed the chaos of a sunny morning.

However, this greyness taught me a sharper definition of gratitude. Because the sun is rare here, I learned to feast on it when it appeared, savoring warmth I used to take for granted. And when it didn't appear? I looked at the locals. They walked through the drizzle with a stoic acceptance, getting wet without getting angry. I learned to adopt that quiet resilience—to accept that while I cannot control the grey ceiling above me, I can control whether I stand under it with resentment or with grace

06. TASTE

Polite Food

In the beginning, food was shame. I’d eat cheap, quickly, and alone, convinced that enjoying a meal was an indulgence that would steal from my future. Back home, food was always loud, always shared, always a connection. Here, it was a silent, purely functional task—a reminder of my isolation.

This shift in perspective finally happened not over an expensive meal, but over a pot of simple, homemade curry. I stopped seeing the cost and started seeing the effort. When I invited my flatmates over and we shared the meal—comparing notes on our separate journeys—the food ceased to be a currency. It became a bridge. I realized that the true nourishment here wasn't in the nutritional facts or the price tag, but in the effort of self-care and the radical act of sharing that effort. The food tasted like home because it was finally seasoned with community, not guilt.

07. BREAKTHROUGH

Raising the Hand

Back home, the classroom was a space built on deference. Silence was the highest form of respect; it signaled deep attention and acceptance of the master’s knowledge. My entire education was built on the discipline of listening. But here, I realized that silence could be a form of self-erasure, mistaken for ignorance. I had to unlearn 19 years of conditioning.

On Sept 7th, I raised my hand to speak. When the professor nodded, something unlocked. My perspective wasn't a deficit; it was an asset. The true meaning was not just in the words I spoke, but in the synthesis. It taught me that real knowledge is not a single river flowing one way, but a conversation that requires both the wisdom to listen and the courage to shape the narrative -- something that was very different back home.

08. SOCIAL

Liquid Courage

The Raven Pub. I learned the British aren't cold; they just need the right room. Alcohol here isn't a vice; it's social glue. For weeks, my life had been a series of rigorous, painful calculations -- monetary, linguistic, and behavioral. I was a perpetually watchful anthropologist, burdened by the need to translate every cost and every gesture.

That night, the mental effort finally collapsed. I laughed -- really laughed. I wasn't calculating currency or monitoring my accent. For the first time since landing, the mental currency converter shut down, and the classroom's demanding focus relaxed. I wasn't an observer or a visitor; I was simply present. That effortless argument about football wasn't about the score; it was the quiet victory of assimilation, a moment where the cultural translation ceased and I was simply a peer.

09. DISSONANCE

The Split Screen

Sept 10th. I sat in the library scrolling through photos of my hometown burning. The cognitive dissonance was physical. This is the diaspora experience: digital bilocation. Your body is safe in wool; your heart is panic-stricken thousands of miles away..

This was, without doubt, the defining moment of my study abroad—a trauma far more challenging than any cultural or academic obstacle. In that paralysis, however, came a devastating clarity. The anger and the shame I felt were simply symptoms of an unstated obligation. I realized that my task was not to abandon my studies out of paralysis or self-pity, but to forge a weapon out of this opportunity. I stopped seeing the future as a choice between a comfortable second-hand existence abroad and the chaos back home. The only true measure of this study abroad will be the work I do when I return.

10. REFUGE

Books and the Arts

But beyond survival, this place taught me to value culture itself. Back home, books were a ladder, a means to an end, a strict path to an exam. It was striking to see the casual reverence here, where people turned to reading for simple comfort when they were merely bored. I realized that the 'obvious knowledge' contained in books was not just for professional advancement, but for personal sustenance

Before Bath, I had never truly visited an art gallery; art was a luxury, random and distant. Here, I discovered its profound utility. I learned that while books provided the 'obvious knowledge,' art offered the 'hidden knowledge'—a complex truth about history and emotion that requires attention.

11. COMMUNITY

Friends along the Way

The social dynamics, too, were a field of study. My group consisted of ten girls, leaving me often feeling like the clear odd one out—not just by gender, but by background. I was always aware of the boundaries and my own limited expectations for connection, often anticipating a respectful, polite distance.

But what I consistently received was not distance, but a conscious, intentional inclusion. They invited me to every spontaneous plan, never allowing my difference to become an excuse for isolation. They didn't just tolerate my presence; they actively chose to integrate me into their world. This taught me a lesson that transcended gender and culture: True community is not a coincidence of sameness; it is a conscious act of empathy. They demonstrated that bridging the small, awkward gaps that divide us requires deliberate effort.

12. Dreams

Dream come True

For years, my connection to the UK was purely digital as I was followed UK YouTubers, absorbing their cultural shorthand and daily routines, feeling connected across a vast, impossible digital gap. My mental map of this country was a mosaic built from vlogs and commentaries

What struck me was the profound accuracy. I finally ate my first Nando's. I visited Old Trafford, the stadium I'd only seen rendered in pixels, and stood where my heroes stood. That first pub experience wasn't just social integration; it was the final confirmation that everything I had absorbed online was authentic.

This was more than just checking items off a list; it was the powerful moment when the digital fantasy I had lived for years was ratified by physical presence. It taught me that global culture is no longer something you passively observe; it’s something you eventually walk into.

13. ADAPTATION

Hybrid Biology

I looked in the mirror and saw a stranger. My face looked sharper. Maybe it was the walking or the stress. My immune system was learning new pathogens; my legs new hills. I wasn't just visiting; the place was entering my biology. I was becoming a hybrid.

But the most profound shift was not physical; it was philosophical. The sharp edges on my face mirrored the new sharpness in my perspective. When I left, the inherent difficulties of a third-world country had led me towards a bitter resentment—a narrow vision of escape. Now, after experiencing the stability and systemic logic of this place, the bitterness had been replaced by a fierce, desperate desire to return and apply every single lesson. I no longer hated the failures of my home; I felt a profound, burning urgency to fix them.

I had become a true hybrid: a product of Nepal's fire and Bath's foundations, carrying the wisdom of both silence and voice. This entire journey was not about finding a new home, but about the necessary, painful process of creating the most capable second edition of myself

14. CONCLUSION

The Hyphen

You never truly leave home, and you never truly arrive. You live in the hyphen. I am Nepali-Student. Here-There. This existence between two strong gravitational fields often felt like a painful limbo

But I have learned that the hyphen isn't a crack in my identity; it is the synthesis. I am not homeless; I am double-homed. This life-in-between is the ultimate vantage point. I see the world from two perspectives—one informed by the necessity of struggle, and the other by the logic of stability. I carry the wisdom of silence and the courage of voice.

This hyphenated existence is not a weakness; it is the ultimate hybrid strength. It is the ability to walk on polished English stone while still remembering every uneven step back home. I am returning, not to escape the hyphen, but to use its double vision to build the future. I am the synthesis of the past and the possible, and that is the only way to build the structure that requires both visions.
